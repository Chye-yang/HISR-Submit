**Abstract.** Sketches provide low-memory-overhead per-flow frequency summaries for high-speed network measurement, but existing methods universally face two intertwined problems: on one hand, hash collisions mix contributions from multiple flows into the same set of counters, making the recovery task essentially a high-noise inverse problem; on the other hand, learned decoders tend to rely on specific traffic distributions, exhibiting significant instability when crossing time windows or deployment conditions. This paper proposes **HISR** (**H**ierarchical **I**nvariant **S**ketch **R**esolution), whose core insight is that what varies across environments is the traffic marginal distribution, while what remains relatively stable is the *measurement structure* jointly induced by hash mappings and additive updates. Building on this insight, HISR does not treat invariance as an add-on regularization term, but rather as a principle that permeates problem decomposition, structural representation, hierarchical decoding, and training objective design. HISR first employs a prefix-based hierarchical decomposition to explicitly exploit prefix-aggregate invariance, then utilizes a key–counter bipartite graph encoder to model hash-induced collision mechanism invariance, and draws on the granularity-based decomposition modeling idea from ZoomSynth to design observation anchoring and a granularity-specialized mass-splitting decoder that injects parent-to-child mass conservation constraints into the output space. During the training phase, we further incorporate multi-environment self-supervised objectives and invariance constraints to suppress the model's reliance on environment-specific shortcuts. Experimental results demonstrate that HISR reduces mean absolute error by 35% relative to the strongest learning-based baseline on real-world network traffic, with only 8–12% performance degradation in unseen environments—significantly outperforming existing methods by 28–35%.
